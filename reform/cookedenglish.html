what is cooked english?

Cooked English (CE; Cóked Énglisç in CE) is a system I designed as a fun little attempt to fix English spelling.

If you have read this, then you know English spelling sucks. This is a representation of what a hypothetical spelling reform can look like.

I do not intend this to be used as an "official" spelling reform, in the sense that I expect people to adapt to it.

Goals:

1. Make English spelling more consistent

2. Maintain word relations. Though etymology is a secondary concern since there are many examples of words getting nativized in the spelling rules of many other languages.


The writing system

CE uses all of the standard 26 letters.

1. New letters

Þ: thorn (þorn). Once used in Old English (alongside Ð, eth) to spell the sounds we now spell as "th": the voiceless and voiced dental fricatives.

Ç: çe (che). Used in French and Portuguese to denote a /s/ before back vowels, and e.g. Turkish to denote /tʃ/. This letter will be used in the same way as in Turkish to write "ch" (/tʃ/).

Ᵹ: ᵹe (je). A variant of the Carolingian "g", called the insular g. Here it is used to write /dʒ/, commonly represented by "g", "dg", and "j".

Ȝ: joȝ (yogh). Derived from the insular g above, once used in Middle English to denote various sounds. Here it represents the remnants of "gh" that has gone silent, and to transcribe the "ch" in Scottish "loch".

Æ: asç (ash). This letter will be used to denote "ea" (and sometimes "a") when pronounced /ɛ/, which is also the same sound it makes in Norwegian.

Ø: øþel (erthel). This letter commonly represents /ø/, but it is similar enough to English /ɝ/ that I will use it to write "o" in -or words when pronounced that way, and to write /ʌ/ in some words.

For Ȝ and Ᵹ, they interchange with G under some related words, e.g. síȝn (sign) but signature.

Optional choices:

Ŋ can be used as an abbreviation for "ng".

Ð can be used as an alternative to Þ.


2. Consonant use changes

J will only be for /j/, with no exceptions. In fact, J comes from the usage of "I" as /j/, and is such in many Germanic and Slavic languages. Additionally, we also have the example of /dʒ/ being spelt as "gi" in Italian, sometimes in words derived from /j/ in Latin itself, just to further my reasons.

G will only be for /g/, so we essentially eliminated the soft g convention. Hard and soft C will be kept.

The sound for "sh" /ʃ/ is now written "SÇ"; by analogy, its voiced equivalent /ʒ/ is written "ZꝽ".

"CH" and "TH" now represents /k/ and /t/ (aspirated or not) respectively in all locations.

d. Other vowel changes

ei = /e, ə, ɪ/ is changed to "e" or "i".

ȩ is used to denote a silent e (that is not a vowel_e combo), and to make the preceding C soft. For example, façade becomes "facȩad", and limaçon becomes "limacȩon".

ê makes the preceding C hard, e.g. Celt becomes "Cêlt".

ocean is spelt "ôsçȩan" to better match with "ôsçéanic" (oceanic).

one is written as "óan".

currently unresolved problems

Not all of U_E is /ju/, in words such as "include", "protrude", etc. Additionally, /ju/ also appears as "u" in some words, such as "occupy", "calculator". This will need a proper representation.

Homographs in CE: e.g. to vs. too, which both become "tó" (I have written "too" as "toó" above, though it is not a perfect solution.)
